Research interests

My research broadly deals with how precipitation systems form, evolve, and impact the general public. This spans cold-season orographic convection contribution to precipipation over complex terrains in arid regions and West African rainfall regimes linked to flood risk. I blend observations, convection-permitting and LES modeling (WRF/MPAS), and climate-model ensembles to connect process-level dynamics with practical forecasting and adaptation.

Precipitation processes: from microscale to large-scale

A core theme is disentangling how stability, gravity waves, terrain-forced lifting, and microphysics organize convection and control precipitation efficiency across scales. I use nested WRF (2.7 km → 900 m → 100 m) and targeted observations to follow the lifecycle of and identify convective cells embedded in stratiform clouds.

Object-based identification of elevated convection (CESC) within stratiform precipitation, corresponding cloud-top heights, and rain/snow rates over complex terrain.
  • CESC case analysis (SNOWIE IOP12): Mesoscale setup and vertical structure of emergent orographic convection; how PI release and terrain interactions focus snowfall. Published (Monthly Weather Review)
  • Precipitation efficiency: Relating cell intensity, organization, and microphysical pathways to distribution of snowfall/downwind spillover.
  • Resolution matters: Downscaling to 100 m LES to capture BL turbulence, waves, and hydrometeor growth not resolved at km-scale.

Convective storms in km-scale climate model simulations

I evaluate how well km-scale models represent convective organization and storm dynamics, using process-oriented diagnostics and satellite/radar constraints. This includes a detection algorithm for CESC and sensitivity-aware metrics that scale to seasonal climatologies and forecast post-processing.

Left: region-growing/labeling pipeline with stability and CTH filters for elevated convection. Right: process-oriented verification linking modeled cells to observed features (GPM/CloudSat/radar).
  • CESC detector: Separate elevated vs. surface-based convection; quantify area, rates, and contributions to total precip.
  • Process-based verification: Connect modeled cell statistics with observed vertical structure and precipitation signatures.
  • Operations pathway: Post-process HRRR/WRF to flag CESC-enhanced snowfall zones for forecasters and seeding programs.

West African rainfall regimes, extremes, and flood potential

I analyze seasonal features like the Little Dry Season (LDS) and quantify heavy-precipitation-induced flood (CHP) potential using multi-index diagnostics with CMIP6 ensembles and CHIRPS validation. Results inform adaptation for urban flooding and water management.

  • LDS dynamics: Historical patterns and drivers; scenario-dependent shifts with spatial heterogeneity. Published (Meteorological Applications)
  • CHP floods: Counts and maxima of meteorological flood potential intensify in Savanna/Sahel; implications for early warning. Published (Scientific African)
  • Next steps: Urbanization–compound risk linkages; CORDEX vs. CMIP6 comparison; refined bias-correction for impacts workflows.
